# 5 Mistakes Homeowners Make Assembling IKEA Furniture (From a Pro Who's Seen It All)

I've assembled IKEA furniture professionally in over 500 homes. I've seen every mistake, every shortcut, and every moment of frustration.

Here are the five mistakes I see most often — and how to avoid them.

---

## Mistake #1: Over-Tightening Cam Locks

Cam locks are the cylindrical fasteners IKEA uses everywhere. They should be turned to "firm" — not "as tight as possible."

Over-tightening splits the particle board around the cam lock housing. Once split, the connection is permanently weakened and can't be repaired without replacement hardware. I see this in about 40% of DIY assemblies I'm called in to fix.

**Fix:** Turn until firm resistance, then stop. The lock symbol on the cam is your guide — when it points toward the dowel, it's engaged. One quarter-turn past that is enough.

---

## Mistake #2: Not Sorting Hardware First

Every IKEA piece comes with multiple hardware types — cam locks, dowels, screws in different sizes, allen bolts, plastic plugs. Most people dump them all out and fish for parts as they go.

This leads to using the wrong screw size, missing a hardware type entirely, and wasting 20 minutes searching through a pile of identical-looking fasteners.

**Fix:** Before you start, sort all hardware into groups by type. IKEA includes more of each piece than you need — that's intentional. Knowing what you have before you start prevents assembly errors.

---

## Mistake #3: Skipping the Back Panel on Shelving Units

On KALLAX, BILLY, and similar shelving units, the back panel (usually thin cardboard or fiberboard) gets installed at the end. Most people rush it or skip it entirely because "it's just the back."

The back panel is structural. It prevents the unit from racking (leaning sideways under load) and keeps the whole piece square. A KALLAX without a properly installed back panel will wobble and eventually deform.

**Fix:** Install the back panel before you put anything on the shelves. Tap it into the channel evenly, start staples or tacks from the middle out, and check that the unit is square before finishing.

---

## Mistake #4: Assembling in the Wrong Room

This sounds obvious, but it happens constantly: assembling a large piece in the living room because there's more space, then discovering it won't fit through the bedroom doorway.

Standard Charlotte doorways are 30–32 inches wide. A PAX wardrobe is 19.5–23.5 inches deep. A KALLAX 4×4 is 57 inches wide. Math matters.

**Fix:** Measure your doorways before assembly, especially for large pieces. If in doubt, do a "door test" with the flat-packed box before you open it. Professionals always assess doorways first.

---

## Mistake #5: Not Wall-Anchoring Tall Units

This is a safety issue, not just an aesthetic one.

Tall shelving units — KALLAX 5×5, BILLY bookcases, PAX wardrobes — need to be anchored to the wall. IKEA includes the hardware and explicitly instructs this in the manual. Most people skip it.

An unanchored 6-foot KALLAX can tip forward under load. With books, it can weigh 200+ pounds. The IKEA anti-tip hardware takes 5 minutes to install and prevents a dangerous situation.

**Fix:** Install the wall anchor as part of assembly, not as an afterthought. Locate a wall stud, use the provided bracket, done.

## When to Call a Pro

If you're facing a PAX wardrobe system, a bunk bed, or multiple large pieces in one session — seriously consider hiring out. The time math almost never works in favor of DIY for complex pieces, and the risk of mistakes is highest exactly when the stakes are highest (expensive furniture, complex assembly, safety-critical pieces).

A good local assembly service will be faster, won't make any of the mistakes above, and will handle cleanup. For many homeowners, that's worth every penny.
